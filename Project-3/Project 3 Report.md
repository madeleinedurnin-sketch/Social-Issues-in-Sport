## Introduction

The rise of social media has fundamentally changed how we respond to sports content, turning historically neutral athletic spaces into complex arenas for public discourse. This project looks at the intersection of sports and politics in modern media, specifically focusing on how public figures are blurring the lines between entertainment, athletics, and political messaging. In an increasingly partisan U.S., politics is often treated as a taboo topic in mixed company. But as people have grown more outspoken, and sometimes hostile, about their political beliefs, the old cultural expectation of "keeping politics out of sports" is constantly being challenged. With social media so deeply baked into everyday life, politicians are now super visible in the exact same content that athletes and fans are making and watching.

Moments that deliberately mash up sports and politics create entirely new digital spaces for public reaction. Donald Trump's guest appearance on golfer Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" YouTube series is a perfect case study of this. It gives us a chance to see how audiences interpret, debate, and negotiate this overlap in the comment sections of different platforms. This case taps into the ongoing debate over whether sports should stay isolated from political ideology or if the two can actually coexist productively. By analyzing highly vocal audiences on both sides of the aisle, we can get a really authentic look at how sport-political integration lands in real time.

Understanding this dynamic is super important. Audience reactions directly shape how sports, media, and politics collide today, influencing athlete reputations, fan perceptions, and broader media narratives. While dropping a polarizing political figure into sports content can definitely spike engagement and views, it carries a massive risk of deep polarization and backlash. Investigating this integration helps sport managers, athletes, and organizations weigh the risks against the strategic opportunities. Driven by all this, our research asks: How do audiences across YouTube, X (formerly Twitter), and Instagram negotiate and react to the blending of sports entertainment and political messaging during Donald Trump's appearance on Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" series?

## Related Literature

**Theoretical Frameworks: Media Presentation and Public Debate**
This project connects to core course concepts around media presentation, public debate, and how different social media platforms drive distinct cultural behaviors. We’re leaning on two main sociological frameworks for our analysis: Framing Theory and Public Sphere Theory. These frameworks help establish a baseline for how audiences interpret and interact with blended content online.

Framing Theory explains how the presentation of content inherently shapes how an audience interprets it. In sports media, content is traditionally framed around athletic performance, skill, and entertainment. But when you drop a political figure like Donald Trump into the mix, that frame immediately expands or fractures. Audiences are forced to figure out how to interpret the video, whether through a purely sports lens, a purely political lens, or some blended version of the two. This active negotiation of frames is key to understanding how a seemingly simple golf video can turn into a lightning rod for partisan debate.

Public Sphere Theory backs this up by highlighting how social media functions as a modern space for public debate. Historically, the public sphere was tied to physical community spaces. Today, the digital public sphere lets strangers interact immediately and without a filter. Platforms like YouTube, X, and Instagram aren't just distribution channels; they're active environments where public opinion is formed, tested, and polarized.

**The Politicization of Non-Political Spaces**
A huge part of our research question is figuring out why audiences react so strongly to political messaging in sports. Wang et al. (2024) really set the stage here by exploring how non-political online spaces get politicized. In their analysis of digital reviews, they found that political discourse routinely bleeds into explicitly non-political domains. As they put it, their findings provide "alarming empirical evidence for the politicization of non-political spaces" (Wang et al., 2024, p. 1).

This maps perfectly onto our look at the "Break 50" series. A golf YouTube channel is usually seen as a completely apolitical zone meant for leisure. However, bringing in a polarizing political figure instantly politicizes that space. Wang et al. (2024) argue that "with identity politics on the rise, this study sheds light on the importance of considering non-political online spaces for the study of political discourse" (p. 1). By analyzing DeChambeau's comment sections, we’re watching this exact phenomenon play out: an entertainment ecosystem morphing into a battleground for political ideology and identity.

**The Phenomenon of the Celebrity Politician**
To make sense of the reactions to Trump in a sports setting, we have to look at his unique spot in the media landscape. Street (2018) explores Trump's specific brand of celebrity politics, noting the assumption that he "has cashed in his success on the reality show The Apprentice to secure political credibility and attention" (p. 3). But Street argues that Trump’s impact is way more complex than just leveraging fame.

Street (2018) suggests that "celebrity politicians like Trump act as stars... They do not just resemble stars, they are them" (p. 3). This is crucial for our research because it explains why Trump showing up on a golf channel feels entirely on-brand for him, yet totally disruptive to traditional sports media. Street adds, "This is evident in how they are represented, how they perform and how their ‘fans’ respond" (2018, p. 3). When audiences react to the Break 50 episode, they aren’t just reacting to a former president playing golf; they’re responding to a superstar celebrity politician doing what he does best: entertaining. This dual identity makes the content inherently hybrid, which is exactly why we see so much blended framing in the comments.

**Identity Conflict in Sport Fandom**
Finally, smashing these political and entertainment identities together inevitably creates friction for the audience. Larkin (2025) digs into this, noting that "despite the natural intersection of sport and politics, it remains an understudied topic in academic literature" (p. 177). Our project directly tackles this gap by looking at how fans handle their allegiances when these two worlds collide.

Larkin (2025) explains that "identity conflict occurs when two salient identities are perceived as incompatible" (p. 177). For politically engaged sport fans, especially those who strongly oppose Trump, his appearance on a golf channel forces an immediate identity crisis. Are they a golf fan first, or a political partisan first? Larkin asks a great question: "Does political identity conflict have the potential to compromise the loyalty of sport fans?" (p. 177). Our cross-platform analysis provides actual data to answer this, showing how fans either ditch their sports identity to jump into political combat or try to aggressively depoliticize the space to protect their fandom. As Larkin (2025) points out, "today, brands feel increasing pressure to take political stands," meaning athletes like DeChambeau are actively navigating the identity conflicts that dictate modern fan loyalty (p. 177).

**Identifying the Gap**
While everyone knows the U.S. is highly polarized, our data bridges a noticeable gap in the literature. We’re tying together Wang et al.'s work on the politicization of neutral spaces, Street's framework of the celebrity politician, and Larkin's concept of fan identity conflict. This project brings real, cross-platform data to the table (comparing Instagram, X, and YouTube) to show exactly how this sport-political crossover is received, and what it actually means for sport organizations considering similar collaborations today.

## Data & Materials

**Dataset Selection and Context**
Our primary data source is user-generated comments pulled from three distinct social media platforms: YouTube, Instagram, and X (formerly Twitter). We specifically focused all our data on audience reactions to a single sport-political moment: Donald Trump's guest appearance on Bryson DeChambeau's "Break 50" YouTube series. Because the data zeroes in on this specific crossover event rather than evaluating either figure's broader social media presence, it gives us a highly targeted, authentic look at how audiences negotiate the deliberate blending of sports and politics. The raw datasets, `B50_INS_COMMENT.xlsx`, `B50_X_COMMENT.xlsx`, and `B50_YT_COMMENT.xlsx`, were pulled right after the episode dropped. The data collection windows reflect the immediate reactionary periods for each platform: July 23 to September 2, 2024, for Instagram; July 23 to August 1, 2024, for X; and July 23 to August 11, 2024, for YouTube.

**Volume and Platform Architecture**
We’re working with a massive volume of data here, 58,464 total comments across the three platforms. Breaking this down by platform shows just how much audience engagement architecture varies:

- **YouTube (45,623 comments):** Generating the bulk of our data, YouTube's setup naturally encourages longer, essay-style replies and deep threaded discussions. This dataset really captures how users interpret the sports-political content, giving us detailed support, criticism, and neutral reactions.
- **Instagram (11,833 comments):** Because it’s a visual-first platform, Instagram drives direct audience interaction that tends to be way more sports-focused and reactive. The comments here are usually shorter, packed with emojis, and generally less overtly political.
- **X / Twitter (1,008 comments):** Even though it’s our smallest dataset, X is built for real-time, text-heavy public discourse. It’s inherently more opinion-driven, reflecting immediate, argumentative reactions and highly polarized debate.
  By combining data from these three totally different social ecosystems, our study benefits from a great mix of comment styles and cultural norms.

**Scope and Inclusion**
To get the full spectrum of audience reactions and keep our own bias in check, we kept the scope super inclusive. We incorporated all rows from each dataset; we didn't drop any platform-level rows during the final analysis. Even comments containing blank strings were kept and coded by the default rules to make sure the total dataset volume stayed perfectly intact. This comprehensive approach lets us analyze the broader distribution of reactions across the highly engaged segment of the audience, rather than just focusing on the loudest voices.

**Data Cleaning and Harmonization**
Getting the data ready for a cross-platform analysis meant standardizing some pretty disparate data structures into shared analytic variables. During the cleaning phase, we took the varying text columns from each platform (like `text` for Instagram and YouTube, and `contents` for X) and harmonized them into a single `comment_text` field. We also created a unified `platform` variable to track where each comment came from.

On top of that, platform-native engagement metrics vary wildly. To fix this, we aggregated them to compute a raw engagement score (`engagement_raw`). For Instagram and YouTube, this included likes and comment replies; for X, it included likes, reply counts, retweets, reference counts, and comment views. Since a single "view" on X is fundamentally different from a "like" on Instagram, we normalized these raw scores using within-platform z-scores.

## Analytical Approach & Justification

**Mixed-Methods Coding Framework**
To really dig into how audiences negotiate meaning, we used a mixed-methods coding approach. Rather than relying entirely on automated sentiment analysis, which honestly struggles to grasp internet culture and niche social slang, we built a hybrid model. This started with an initial dictionary-based detection script, followed by context-sensitive social rules, and ended with iterative, user-guided calibration.

This approach categorized each comment across two primary dimensions: **Political Stance** and **Interpretive Frame**.

* **Political Stance** was broken down into three categories: `pro-Trump`, `anti-Trump`, and `neutral/non-political`. The base dictionary logic scanned for supportive cues, campaign slogans, and praise to classify comments as `pro-Trump`. On the flip side, it scanned for critical cues, accusations of criminality, and explicit anti-campaign messaging to classify comments as `anti-Trump`. Comments that didn't trip these specific triggers defaulted to `neutral/non-political`.
* **Interpretive Frame** looked at the primary thematic lens the commenter used. It was operationalized as `sports/performance` (identifying golf and performance vocab) or `politics/ideology` (identifying election, office, or party terms). If a comment hit keywords from *both* dictionaries, it was classified as `blended`.

To give you an idea of the exact lexical triggers used in the initial detection script, here is a chart outlining the core keywords and regular expressions mapped to their classifications:

| Analytical Category          | Classification         | Core Keywords & Lexical Patterns                                                                                                                                                |
| :--------------------------- | :--------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Political Stance**   | `pro-Trump`          | *maga, potus, trump 2024, vote trump, president trump, former president trump, go trump, team trump, thank trump, trump won, 45 & 47, 47th president, president 47, 47 again* |
| **Political Stance**   | `anti-Trump`         | *felon, rapist, racist, dictator, traitor, anti-trump, fuck trump, ban trump, no trump, never trump, criminal, do not vote trump, not voting for trump, domestic terrorist*   |
| **Interpretive Frame** | `sports/performance` | *golf, break 50, break 47, round, swing, driver, putt, putter, birdie, eagle, par, fairway, green, shot, game, play, score, course, bryson*                                   |
| **Interpretive Frame** | `politics/ideology`  | *trump, potus, president, former president, election, vote, maga, gop, democrat, republican, leftist, rightwing, liberal, conservative, political, 47th president*            |

*Note: The `blended` frame is computationally derived when a comment triggers both `sports/performance` and `politics/ideology` keywords at the same time.*

This setup was super critical. Because our research question centers on the *blending* of sports and politics, we absolutely needed a dedicated "blended" category to track users who actively merged the two lenses, rather than forcing them into a strict binary choice.

**Resolving Ambiguity Through User Calibration**
A major flaw with purely dictionary-based methods is that they totally miss implied meaning, complex irony, or highly specific cultural inside jokes. To fix this, we ran the analysis through two rounds of explicit user-calibrated ambiguity resolution.

During Round 1, we set up specific heuristics: numbers like "47" or "45&47" were coded as `pro-Trump` even without explicit name drops. Conversely, generic positive references to "president" or "American" without explicit Trump cues defaulted to `neutral`. When comments had mixed positive and negative cues (like "MAGA... domestic terrorists"), the negative cue took priority to capture the sarcastic or critical intent (`anti-Trump`).

Round 2 fine-tuned this contextual understanding even more. For example, the phrase "Make America Great Again" was handled dynamically: if a golf or sports context was present, it was coded as `pro-Trump` and `blended`; if it was just isolated text, it was categorized as purely `politics/ideology`. Similarly, sports-adjacent praise like "GOAT" or "king" defaulted to a `blended` frame when there were no explicit political markers, reflecting the hybrid nature of the praise. Finally, we built explicit logic to handle sarcasm markers (like "lol," "sure," "/s") which are famously difficult for automated systems to catch. To audit this process and make sure it was reproducible, we generated transition matrices between passes to document the exact count and percentage of changed labels.

**Engagement Normalization and Justification**
A core part of our analysis involved measuring the magnitude of audience reactions. But cross-platform comparability is a massive headache because engagement architectures differ so wildly. An engagement metric that means "viral success" on X might mean something completely different on Instagram.

To fix this, we first computed a raw score (`engagement_raw`) by aggregating the available platform-native signals: likes and replies for YouTube and Instagram, and likes, reply counts, retweets, reference counts, and comment views for X. To actually compare these across platforms, we applied a logarithmic transformation (`log1p(engagement_raw)`) to handle extreme outliers, followed by a within-platform z-score calculation (`engagement_norm`). This methodological choice is heavily justified because it standardizes the magnitude of engagement relative to each platform's unique baseline. It ensures that X's massive view counts don't artificially overshadow Instagram's metric structure in our final analysis.

**Statistical Testing Strategy**
For our quantitative analysis, we relied exclusively on non-parametric statistical tests. Because online engagement variables are notoriously non-normally distributed, meaning they are highly skewed and heavily zero-inflated (where tons of comments get zero replies or likes), running parametric tests like ANOVAs would violate core statistical assumptions.

Instead, to look at categorical differences (like cross-platform stance distribution, frame distribution, and stance-by-frame associations), we used Chi-square tests of independence, backed up by standardized residuals and Cramer's V to measure effect size.

To evaluate continuous engagement differences, like whether blended comments actually generated more engagement than non-blended ones, we used the Mann-Whitney U test paired with Cliff's delta for effect size. For comparing engagement across the three stances, we ran a Kruskal-Wallis test, followed by post-hoc pairwise Mann-Whitney tests with Bonferroni corrections to keep Type I errors in check.

**Qualitative Interpretive Layer**
Finally, to ground all these statistical findings in the reality of the actual discourse, we added an interpretive qualitative layer. We pulled representative comment excerpts from within each `stance x frame` matrix cell. To make sure we got quality and relevance, we prioritized higher-engagement comments, scrubbed duplicates, and explicitly favored comments with clear lexical alignment to their assigned category. This mixed-methods triangulation ensures that the statistical trends we’re reporting are directly tied to the actual language the fans used.

## Results

Our quantitative analysis yielded several key findings surrounding platform distribution, frame-stance association, and engagement differentials.

**Stance and Framing Distributions Across Platforms**
Across all three platforms, `sports/performance` was the dominant interpretive frame, and the highest proportion of comments remained `neutral/non-political`. However, X (formerly Twitter) displayed a very different distribution compared to YouTube and Instagram. A Chi-square test of independence revealed that X had a significantly larger share of both explicitly political stances and `politics/ideology` framing than the other two platforms. In terms of directional valence, `pro-Trump` frequency outpaced `anti-Trump` frequency across all three datasets.

**The Association Between Stance and Frame**
The data demonstrated a statistically significant association between political stance and interpretive frame. Neutral comments were overwhelmingly concentrated within the `sports/performance` frame. `Pro-Trump` comments were observed most frequently within the `blended` frame. Conversely, `anti-Trump` comments were most heavily concentrated in pure `politics/ideology` frames rather than the `sports/performance` or `blended` categories.

**Engagement Differentials**
A Mann-Whitney U test confirmed that `blended` comments, those containing both sports and political lexical cues, yielded statistically significantly higher normalized engagement than non-blended comments. Furthermore, a Kruskal-Wallis analysis of engagement by political stance, followed by post-hoc pairwise testing, revealed some interesting platform-specific patterns. On X, explicitly political comments generated the highest engagement metrics. On YouTube and Instagram, comments classified under a mixed-sports framework performed the highest. Overall, explicitly political and blended comments achieved higher raw visibility metrics than strictly neutral commentary.

## Interpretation & Discussion

The data clearly indicates that audiences actively negotiate meaning when consuming hybrid content, utilizing both political and sports frames simultaneously. This negotiation isn't uniform or arbitrary; it’s heavily shaped by platform architecture, audience identity, and the intentional blending of the "celebrity politician" persona.

**Platform Culture as a Modifier of the Public Sphere**
As demonstrated in **Figure 1 (Stance Proportions Across Platforms)** and **Figure 2 (Frame Composition Across Platforms)**, the "public sphere" is definitely not monolithic. While sports/performance frames dominated overall, X (formerly Twitter) acted as an accelerant for ideological debate, fostering a significantly higher proportion of explicitly political comments than YouTube or Instagram. This directly supports Public Sphere Theory by showing that varying platform cultures act as distinct, fragmented spaces for negotiation. It also aligns perfectly with Wang et al.’s (2024) point that the politicization of non-political spaces depends heavily on the algorithmic and cultural affordances of the specific platform. On Instagram, the highly curated, visual nature of the app seemingly protected the "golf aesthetic," suppressing political discourse compared to the inherently text-driven, combative ecosystem of X.

**The Co-Production of Stance and Frame**
**Figure 3 (Stacked Frame-by-Stance Plot)** provides a really critical insight into how political ideology dictates interpretive framing. The data reveals that `pro-Trump` comments were heavily tied to a `blended` frame, whereas `anti-Trump` comments retreated into purely `politics/ideology` frames. This maps seamlessly onto Street’s (2018) concept of the "superstar celebrity politician." Trump’s supporters were able to easily blend his political brand with his entertainment value, celebrating his golf performance as just another extension of his political vitality. In contrast, critical audiences largely rejected the sports premise entirely, dragging the discourse back into a purely political and moral framework. In the context of Framing Theory, it shows that audiences do not passively accept the "golf" frame offered by DeChambeau; they actively reconstruct the frame to fit their partisan needs.

**The Economics of Polarization and Identity Conflict**
**Figure 4 (Boxplot of Normalized Engagement by Frame Type)** and **Figure 5 (Engagement by Stance Category)** reveal the underlying economic engine of this crossover content: polarization is profitable. Comments that actively blended sports and politics generated significantly higher engagement than those that isolated themselves to a single frame. This high traction for blended and explicitly political comments indicates that sport-political integration doesn't just polarize an audience; it actively drives outsized public interaction.

This phenomenon is best explained by Larkin’s (2025) framework of identity conflict among sport fans. When a highly polarizing political figure enters a traditionally insulated sports space, it forces fans to reconcile competing loyalties. The resulting cognitive dissonance and identity threat drive users to the comment section to aggressively validate or defend their worldview. Consequently, the algorithm rewards this conflict with elevated visibility.

**Qualitative Thematic Interpretation**
Beyond the statistical trends, **Figure 6 (Integrated Findings)** grounds this data in reality by identifying the four dominant qualitative narratives audiences used to negotiate this crossover:

1. **The Celebration Narrative:** Supportive users treated Trump's appearance as a source of legitimacy and high entertainment value, seamlessly blending praise for his golf swing with political support.
2. **The Backlash Narrative:** Critical users deliberately interrupted sports-focused discussions to inject political context, aggressively rejecting the "Break 50" premise to center moral critique.
3. **The Depoliticization Narrative:** A sizable cohort attempted to artificially protect their fandom by actively ignoring the political reality, focusing hyper-specifically on DeChambeau's production, scoring, and gameplay.
4. **The Irony/Banter Narrative:** Users across platforms deployed memes, short-form slang, and sarcasm to participate in the spectacle without committing to a deeply serious ideological stance.

**Managerial Implications**
For sport managers, content creators, and athletes, these findings highlight a critical reality: collaborating with political figures is a high-risk, high-visibility strategy. It guarantees a hijacking of the comment section and generates profound polarization, but it simultaneously drives massive engagement metrics by triggering identity conflict. Success or damage control in these scenarios requires understanding platform-specific cultures, because a viral, highly political comment on X represents a fundamentally different PR challenge than an engagement spike on Instagram.

## Ethical Reflection & Limitations

**Methodological Limitations**
While this study provides some really robust, cross-platform insights into the blending of sports and politics, we have to acknowledge a few methodological limitations. First, our dataset is essentially a static snapshot captured during the immediate reactionary window right after the "Break 50" episode was released. Online discourse around polarizing political figures is incredibly fluid. Because of this, our study can't capture how public perception or platform narratives evolved over time as the video got reshared or as the broader political news cycle shifted.

Second, analyzing the dataset relied heavily on dictionary methods and rule-based coding, which inherently struggle with the nuances of internet culture. Comments often use implied meaning, deep sarcasm, and inside jokes that simple text classification can miss or miscategorize. For example, users frequently hijacked sports slang to make political points, and vice versa. While our user-guided calibration rounds mitigated a lot of this, some level of ambiguity definitely remains. Furthermore, cross-platform comparisons are inherently complex; the fundamental difference between an engagement metric on X versus an engagement metric on Instagram means we are comparing structurally distinct architectures, even after normalization.

**Sample Bias**
It’s also critical to acknowledge sample bias here. The data reflects only a highly engaged minority of users who felt compelled enough to leave a public comment. The reactions of the "silent majority" who merely watched the video without interacting remain unknown. Additionally, the baseline audience inherently skews toward existing golf enthusiasts and Bryson DeChambeau subscribers, meaning these reactions aren't perfectly representative of the general public.

**Ethical Considerations and Privacy**
Because this research analyzes the deeply personal and often inflammatory political ideologies of private users, we had to establish strict ethical boundaries. While the datasets consist of user-generated comments from publicly accessible posts on Instagram, X, and YouTube, they originally contained identifiable metadata. To treat the data confidentially and protect user privacy, identifying markers like usernames were completely stripped prior to analysis. This ensures that individual users cannot be targeted or doxed based on their political expressions within our dataset.

**Mitigating Harm and Researcher Bias**
Beyond data privacy, a major ethical challenge involved the risk of amplifying the inflammatory rhetoric or partisan vitriol present in the data. The intersection of politics and sport, especially when involving a highly polarizing figure like Donald Trump, naturally elicits heated and sometimes toxic discourse. Generalizing these findings without caution could inadvertently reinforce negative stereotypes about golf enthusiasts or users of specific platforms.

We navigated this challenge by maintaining strict academic objectivity. The analysis was entirely focused on *how* audiences negotiate meaning and boundaries, rather than validating, judging, or giving an unfiltered platform to extreme political sentiments. We also actively acknowledged the risk of researcher bias. Because political comments are super subjective, we took great care during the manual calibration phase to avoid projecting our own political leanings onto the thematic coding, ensuring the final interpretations remained firmly grounded in the data.
